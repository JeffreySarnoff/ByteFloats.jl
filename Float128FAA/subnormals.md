Does fma128 work correctly with a two normal multiplicands and a subnormal addend?

Short answer from the design: yes to all three — decompose normalizes subnormal inputs into (significand, extended exponent) pairs (down to e = −16494), the 256-bit accumulator has no exponent floor, and results re-enter the subnormal grid through the pre-round jam-shift, so subnormals are handled uniformly rather than as special cases. The suite already exercised this (the subnormal-heavy battery and the underflow-threshold sweep), but your three questions deserve targeted, high-volume verification against native fmaq. Validated fma128's subnormal handling through decomposition and empirical testing.

Yes to all three — each verified just now with ~6.8 million freshly targeted cases, every one bit-identical to native fmaq, on top of the original suite's subnormal batteries. Here's why each works mechanically:

All-subnormal values (Q1). decompose erases the normal/subnormal distinction at the door: a subnormal's fraction is shifted left until its top bit sits at position 112 and the exponent extends below e_min (down to −16494 for the smallest subnormal). From there the pipeline is uniform — the 256-bit accumulator has no exponent floor. A product of two subnormals is below 2^−32764, always under half the smallest subnormal, so it correctly underflows; the sign is preserved (±0 per round-to-nearest), and any subnormal addend takes over via the alignment logic. 2M random all-subnormal triples: zero mismatches.

Subnormal × subnormal + normal addend (Q2). The exact product lands at exponent ≈ −32988…−32764, while a normal z sits at ≥ −16382 — an alignment gap of at least ~16,000 bits. The capped shift-right-jam collapses the product to a single sticky bit at position 0, far below the guard bit at 141, so the result is RN(z ± ε) with ε below half an ulp: z itself for effective addition, and for effective subtraction the borrow ripples through the accumulator, renormalizes, and rounds back up to z — including the delicate case where z is a power of two and the borrow crosses a binade. 2M cases including z pinned at the bottom of the normal range: zero mismatches.

Normal × normal + subnormal addend (Q3). This is the genuinely deep case, because two small normals can produce a product inside the subnormal result range (exponents down to −32764), where the subnormal z's bits fully overlap the rounding window rather than vanishing into sticky. That's handled by the subnormal pre-shift before rounding: when the tentative exponent field is ≤ 0, the accumulator is jam-shifted onto the subnormal grid and rounded there, with the min-normal rounding carry falling out of the bit assembly automatically. I steered 1.5M cases so the product exponent sum lands in [−16650, −16250], plus 500k cancellation cases z = −(x⊗y) with subnormal residuals, plus the full-range population: zero mismatches, and the directed grid of boundary atoms (min/max subnormal, floatmin, √floatmin, ±0) is likewise clean.

One caveat for completeness: "correct" here means bit-identical to IEEE correctly-rounded fmaq on Linux, cross-confirmed by the BigFloat nearest/ties-to-even oracle in the original suite. Floating-point flags (underflow/inexact) are not modeled — if ByteFloats ever needed flag semantics rather than values, that would be the one gap.
